Week 9

What Is Grounded Theory?

  • A methodology for building theory inductively from data — not testing existing theory
  • Developed by Glaser and Strauss (1967); Charmaz's constructivist version is widely used in humanistic research
  • Iterative: collect data, code, theorize, collect more data, refine codes
  • Result: a theory that is grounded in what participants actually say and do
Grounded theory is particularly useful when you genuinely don't know what you'll find — it rewards letting the data surprise you.
Week 9 — Saldaña

The Coding Process

Open coding: assign labels to every meaningful passage — no preconceived categories, stay close to the data
Axial coding: look for relationships between codes — what clusters? What causes or enables what?
Selective coding: identify the central category — what concept integrates the others?
Theoretical coding: write a theory — a claim about how the categories relate

A code is not a label slapped on text — it's an interpretation of what the passage is doing.

Week 9 — Charmaz

Constructivist Grounded Theory (Charmaz 2014)

  • Neither data nor theory are discovered — they are constructed through researcher–data interaction
  • Researcher's perspective, assumptions, and context shape what gets coded
  • Reflexivity is built into the method — document your analytical decisions
  • Memo-writing is essential: write about your codes, not just with them
