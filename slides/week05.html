Week 5 — Brock

Critical Technocultural Discourse Analysis (CTDA)

  • Examines how technology and culture co-constitute each other
  • Attends to how race, power, and identity are embedded in interface design and platform affordances
  • Not just "what does the content say" — also "what does the platform allow, foreground, or suppress?"
  • Discourse at the level of the platform itself, not just user-generated text
Week 5 — Noble

Algorithms of Oppression (Noble 2018)

  • Search algorithms are not neutral information retrieval tools
  • Commercial logic shapes what appears — and what is marginalized
  • Noble: searching for "Black girls" returned harmful results — this is a methodological problem, not just a content problem
  • For researchers: using search as a data collection method requires accounting for algorithmic bias
Week 5 — Carah & Dobson

Algorithmic Hotness

  • Platforms reward certain kinds of bodies and aesthetics with visibility
  • "Hotness" as a platform affordance — content optimized for engagement often reproduces normative identity performance
  • Method: track what gets amplified and theorize what that amplification logic encodes
This is platform analysis, not content analysis — the unit of study is the visibility logic, not the individual posts.
Week 5 — Fiesler & Proferes

Ethics of Social Media Research

  • Users often don't know their public posts are being used for research
  • Perception of "publicness" varies — Twitter users may not expect academic analysis
  • AoIR guidelines apply: consider context, vulnerability, power dynamics
  • Even publicly available data requires ethical consideration of how it's used
