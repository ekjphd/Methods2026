Week 4

What Is Archival Research?

  • Using existing records — documents, images, data — as primary sources
  • Sources were created for purposes other than your research question
  • Includes physical archives, digitized collections, born-digital repositories, data archives
  • Key question: who created this record, for what purpose, and what did they leave out?
Archives are not neutral repositories — they reflect the priorities of the institutions and people who built them.
Week 4

Archival Design Types

  • Historical analysis — documents, letters, newspapers, legal records
  • Content analysis — systematic coding of texts or images in the archive
  • Secondary data analysis — data collected by others (census, government surveys)
  • Digital archives — born-digital sources, web archives, social media datasets
Week 4 — Owens

Digital Preservation (Owens 2018)

  • Digital preservation ≠ just keeping files alive — it's maintaining access, context, and authenticity over time
  • Formats degrade; platforms disappear; links break
  • Before using a digital source as data: assess its provenance, format history, and curation decisions
  • What decisions were made about what to digitize? Who funded that work?
For DH and Public History students especially: the "digital" in digital archives is itself a methodological decision.
Week 4

Coding Archival Data (Vogt et al. 2014)

  • Develop a coding scheme before analyzing (or use grounded approach)
  • Document your decisions — what counts as an instance of each code?
  • Intercoder reliability: have more than one person code a sample
  • Track what you cannot code — absences and gaps are data too
