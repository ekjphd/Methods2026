Week 7 — Interviews

Why Interview?

  • Access to participants' interpretations, experiences, and meanings
  • Flexible — can follow unexpected threads
  • Semi-structured: prepared questions + room to probe
  • What a survey can't capture: tone, hesitation, elaboration, contradiction
IRB must approve your interview questions — but you can still probe and follow up on anything the participant raises.
Week 7

Interview Best Practices (Lessons Learned)

  • As the interviewer: just shut up. Silence is productive.
  • Encourage rambling and elaboration — "tell me more about that"
  • Identify main questions vs. sub-questions you can skip if already answered
  • Don't be afraid of open questions: "Tell me about your experience with this"
  • Don't be afraid of follow-ups: "Why?" "What specifically?" "You mentioned X — can you say more?"
  • Verify and clarify: "So when you say X, you mean…?"
Week 7

Interview Limitations

  • Interviews are conducted with people by people — social desirability, power dynamics, perceived expectations
  • Difference (real or perceived) between interviewer and interviewee affects honesty
  • This is not a reason to discount interviews — it goes in your limitations section
"Interestingly, I noticed that during interviews most of the librarians lowered their voices when saying the words 'gay' or 'transgender'… This discomfort is not something that could have been conveyed through statistics in a quantitative survey." — Linda Garrison, T&T dissertation
Week 7 — Observation

Observational Design Types

  • Non-participant observation — researcher watches without interacting
  • Participant observation — researcher joins the group/activity
  • Naturalistic — behavior in its natural context, no intervention
  • Structured — pre-defined categories, systematic recording
  • Action research — researcher is also an actor in the setting
Fieldnotes are the data — what you notice, how you describe it, what you leave out are all methodological choices.
